1. ## **Background of the study** 
What happens when humanity is disarmed by a world overrun by zombies? Will the world still be fine? The researchers presented a series named “*The Walking Dead.”* The Walking Dead is a television series adapted from the comic book of the same name, which raises complex themes of power, fear, and control in a post-apocalyptic world. The series depicts the characters' struggle to survive in a world devastated by a zombie plague, where individuals and groups must struggle to maintain power and survive (Ambrosius & Valenzano III, 2016). However, one of the main issues that arises in the series is how power and fear are manipulated to control individuals and groups (Kennedy, n.d.). This phenomenon illustrates how fear can be used to create dominance and regulate social structures in extreme situations, whose relevance can be found in various social and political dynamics in the real world.

One of the most striking symbols that depicts the dynamics of power and fear is Lucille, a wooden bat used by Negan, the leader of the group, The Saviors. Lucille is not just a physical weapon but also a symbol of power gained through fear. The fear created by Lucille as a punitive tool serves to reinforce Negan's dominance over other groups. The main problem that arises is how this fear not only affects the characters in the story but also reflects the dynamics of power in the wider society, where symbols of power can create or reinforce social hierarchies (YOUNG, 2023). In the real world, we often see symbols of power that function similarly, where tools of power, such as weapons, money, or influence, are used to control and strengthen existing social structures.

<a name="_zc6pxgebj6d"></a>Semiotics is a discipline that studies symbols and their functions in diverse contexts. Maulida Laily Kusuma Wati et al., (2023) Semiotic theory provides a powerful guide to understanding how symbols such as Lucille work in the construction of meaning and how these symbols are used to shape and control the perception of the audience. Ferdinand de Saussure suggests that a sign consists of two main components: signifiers and signifieds. A sign is the physical or material form of a sign, while a sign is a concept or meaning associated with the sign (de Saussure, 2011). Roland Barthes is a figure who continues Saussure's thinking.  Saussure put his attention and interest on how the complex way of sentence formation and the way sentences form in choosing meaning, but he was less interested that in fact the same sentence or message conveyed could have different meanings to people who were in different situations. Denotation meaning is the real meaning. The connotative meaning is the figurative meaning of a word. Meanwhile, myth is a form of message or a standard speaker of the community in its time that must be believed to be true without having to be proven (Afrisyah & Kurniawan, 2024). In the context of *The Walking Dead*, Lucille can be seen as a sign that has denotation, connotation, and myth according to Roland Barthes' semiotic analysis. Negan uses Lucille as a weapon, a baseball bat wrapped in barbed wire. However, Lucille also symbolizes Negan's power, dominance, and fear, so the bat is not just a weapon but a symbol of violence and psychological control. Lucille is not only a physical tool, but also a narrative tool that confirms Negan's position as a tyrannical leader. This dynamic reflects how symbols in society can serve to strengthen the power system through cultural associations and influences. It is important to note that in *The Walking Dead*, the power dynamics shaped by symbols like Lucille not only reflect conditions in a fictional world but also reflect social realities in the real world. Fear of being manipulated to maintain power can be seen in various forms in modern society, both in political, social, and economic relations (Zizek, 2008). Lucille describes how individuals or groups who have control over the tools of power, be it weapons, money, or influence, can use fear to reinforce their dominance and control others (Wolfman, 2023). This representation of fear through symbols not only has an impact on the characters in The Walking Dead but also provides a lesson on how fear can be used in power structures in the real world.

Comparisons with other literary works that also use symbols of power and fear can be made through classics such as 1984 by George Orwell. In this novel, power and control are represented by more direct symbols, such as *the Big Brother*, who not only watches but also creates fear in order to control their people. 1984 depicts how a totalitarian regime can exploit fear to manipulate reality and control individuals through invisible surveillance, very similar to how *The Walking Dead* uses physical symbols such as Lucille to create fear that perpetuates power. While 1984 focused more on power in a broader political and social context, *The Walking Dead* depicts power in a post-apocalyptic situation, where fear is used to sustain surviving groups through brutal influence and physical force. Both of these works show how fear and symbols of power can be used to maintain dominance, albeit against very different backgrounds.

In addition, in modern literary works such as Lord of the Flies by William Golding, symbols of power are also very prominent, with the use of objects such as horns that represent authority and fear used to control groups. As in *The Walking Dead*, this fear creates a sharp divide between the powerful and the oppressed, showing how these symbols control the actions of individuals and groups in extreme situations. As such, both Lord of the Flies and *The Walking Dead* illustrate how fear and power can worsen social conditions, creating hierarchies that are detrimental to weaker parties. This study is important because it helps uncover how symbols of power, such as the Lucille, can function in creating fear, controlling individuals, and maintaining social hierarchies. In a broader context, understanding how fear is produced and maintained through symbols can help us to be more critical in looking at how power works in our society. By observing how *The Walking Dead* portrays the manipulation of fear through Lucille, we can gain deeper insights into the dynamics of power in real life, as well as how we can be more wary of practices that use fear to control others (Adolph, 2016).

1. ## **Definition of Terminology**
To avoid misunderstanding in this study, the writer provides an explanation of the terms used in the study as follows:

1. **Power Dynamics**

   According to Michel Foucault (1995), power dynamics refer to how power is distributed and applied in social relations. Power not only flows from top to bottom but is spread in various forms of social interaction, be it in individual, group, or institutional relationships. In the media context, power dynamics refers to how the characters or groups in a story play a role in defending, challenging, or dominating each other's power. In *The Walking Dead*, power dynamics are related to how characters like Negan use power to control other groups and how other characters respond and try to reclaim that power.

1. **Fear Representation** 

   Refers to how the media or narrative describes the experience of fear felt by a character or audience. Susan Sontag (2004) suggests that fear in the media is often used to describe a more significant threat to social or individual well-being. In *The Walking Dead*, fear is portrayed visually and narratively through the threat of zombies and authoritarian rulers like Negan. This representation of fear occurs not only through physical tension but also in the psychological and emotional tension experienced by the characters involved.

1. **Semiotics**\
   Ferdinand de Saussure (1916) explains that semiotics is the study of the signs and symbols that form meaning in language and communication. Semiotics investigates how signs (such as words, images, sounds, or actions) shape perceptions and meanings in culture. In the context of *The Walking Dead*, semiotics focuses on how visual images, dialogue, and objects (such as weapons or Lucille, Negan's baseball bat) are used to convey key themes, such as power and fear, and form a deeper meaning of the narrative.

1. **Semiotics**

Semiotics is the study of signs and symbols in human communication, exploring how meaning is created and understood. It examines various theories to reveal how these elements convey meaning and influence interactions, highlighting their cultural and societal significance. The concept of modern semiotics is rooted in the thought of Ferdinand de Saussure, who defined signs as the relationship between signifiers and signifieds. Roland Barthes' semiotic theory, which has significantly impacted literary and cultural studies, asserts that signs consist of a signifier (the form of the sign) and a signified (the concept it represents). Barthes further differentiates between denotation (the literal meaning) and connotation (the cultural or emotional associations), stressing that meanings are not static but are shaped by cultural contexts and social practices (Pramudiyanto et al., n.d., 2025). Barthes most important extension is the concept of Myth, in which signs operate at a second level, transforming connotative meanings into ideological narratives that are considered natural or absolute in society. This perspective resonates with contemporary discussions across various fields, such as analyzing material-semiotic environments in health research, which emphasizes the interplay between physical and symbolic elements in shaping experiences and narratives, particularly in rehabilitation contexts (Monforte et al., 2020). Additionally, the application of semiotic theories in archaeology enhances Barthes' framework, bridging gaps in the understanding of the relationship between materiality and representation in cultural artifacts. (Swenson & Cipolla, 2020).

1. **Power Dynamics in Literature**

Michel Foucault's theory of power offers a nuanced exploration of how power dynamics shape interpersonal relationships, a concept that resonates in fictional narratives found in films and TV series. Foucault asserts that power is both relational and pervasive, functioning through knowledge and social structures rather than solely through authority or position (Ladkin & Probert, 2021). This perspective is vividly illustrated in narratives such as Dave Eggers' "The Circle," where a technology company manipulates digital information to exert control over individuals, thereby transforming societal power structures (Paramagururaj & Subha, 2023). Additionally, Foucault's analysis of biopower reveals how cultural artifacts, including pornography, regulate individual behaviors and identities, reflecting broader societal norms and power relations (Kurylo, 2017). These insights highlight the significance of Foucault's theories in understanding contemporary media representations and their implications for individual autonomy and societal governance (Metzger, 2016).

1. **Representation of Fear**

In many works, fear is often conveyed through objects that have symbolic meaning. Therefore, it is important to understand how the concept of representation works.
### <a name="_gjbkbfyc72fd"></a>**Theory of Representation**
Representation, in its broad theoretical sense, refers to the process by which certain entities (such as images, objects, or linguistic expressions) present or stand for particular contents and meanings. (Plagnol, 2023) Representation works through two complementary elements,  the analogous aspect, which presents the content directly, and the symbolic aspect, which requires an intermediary through the process of interpretation. This process is firmly rooted in semiotics, where signs serve as a mechanism for creating and communicating meaning (Saussure, 1983). Its basic structure depends on the relationship between signifiers and *signifieds*, where signifiers (physical forms) represent signifiers (concepts) (Saussure, 1983).  This mechanism of representation facilitates the individual's access to abstract ideas, feelings, and concepts that are outside of direct experience by linking symbolic frameworks to perceptual or cultural meanings. Further, in cultural and media contexts, representation involves different levels of meaning, from denotation (literal) to connotation (cultural association), which shape how a concept is perceived (Barthes, 1967). In the field of literature and visual media, objects often act as representational entities whose significance exceeds their physical form, thus being able to communicate emotional, psychological, or thematic nuances. Understanding this theoretical foundation is crucial before examining how fear is represented through objects, given that the emotional influence of these objects is highly dependent on the process of representation that combines material existence with symbolic content.

The representation of fear through objects, especially those related to antagonistic characters, is a multifaceted concept that is explored in various forms of media and literature. In horror cinema, objects of disgust and violence, such as guns, play an important role in inducing fear by eliciting a rational and emotional response (Sopuck, 2021). This is in line with the idea that objects can manifest fear through the presence of matter and their symbolic associations. Jeong-ok Oh's study further elaborates on this by examining how elements such as "fire" and "water" in folklore are not only the environment but also serve as archetypal symbols of fear and death, enhancing the terrifying qualities of the narrative when combined with bewitching entities such as tigers (Oh, 2022). In literature, as seen in Stephen King's analysis of "It", fear is amplified through narrative techniques that make the impossible scary, while cinematic adaptations use visual elements to evoke fear in the listener (Costa & Sales Araújo, 2021). The affective aesthetics of fictional objects, such as those explored by Alaa Ali, highlight how objects in contemporary fiction can evoke fear through their association with bad themes, thus creating a triangular relationship between influence, aesthetics, and objects (Ramos, 2022). Historical perspectives on materiality and emotions, including fear, across different cultures and time periods, further underscore their role as "emotional objects" with agency (Downes et al., 2018). Collectively, these studies illustrate how objects, whether through physical presence, symbolic meaning, or narrative function, serve as powerful conduits for fear, reinforcing their role in literature and visual media.
